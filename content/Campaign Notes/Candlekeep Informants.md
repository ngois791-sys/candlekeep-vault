# Candlekeep Informants

_Three lower-ranking Avowed who have each noticed something wrong — from completely different angles._

> [!important] DM Eyes Only These three NPCs are the party's internal breadcrumb trail through the conspiracy. They arrive staggered across the three acts. They know each other casually — the way coworkers do — but have not connected their individual concerns until the party brings them together. Handle each one privately at first.

## Pell Ashwick

_Male halfling | Mid-30s | Copyist, House of the Binder | Arrives: Act One_

---

### Appearance & Personality

Pell is small even for a halfling — neat, precise, and almost aggressively unremarkable. He keeps his dark hair combed flat, his robes well-pressed, and his ink-stained fingers tucked out of sight when he's nervous, which is often. He has the posture of someone who has spent fifteen years hunched over manuscripts and the eyes of someone who notices everything.

He is not naturally brave. He is naturally thorough. Those two things are currently in direct conflict, and it shows.

> [!note] At the Table Pell speaks quickly when anxious and goes very still when he's said something he thinks he shouldn't have. He uses precise language — he will correct himself mid-sentence if he's overstated something. He doesn't exaggerate. If he says something is wrong, it is wrong.

---

### Role in Candlekeep

Pell has worked in the House of the Binder for fifteen years, copying manuscripts by hand. He is one of their most reliable scribes — meticulous, fast, and discreet. His job gives him access to copy requests from across the entire keep, which means he sees what texts are being requested, by whom, and how often.

He has noticed that certain books being ordered for copying don't match anything in the Candlekeep catalogue. Someone is requesting copies of texts that officially don't exist in the collection. The requests come through legitimate Avowed channels — but the authorisation signatures are subtly wrong. Not forged, exactly. More like... rubber-stamped without real attention. As if whoever approved them didn't read what they were approving.

He has been sitting on this for three months. He compiled a list. He didn't know who to bring it to until the party appeared — outsiders, not Avowed, not part of whatever is happening.

---

### What He's Noticed — Timeline

**Early Act One:** Pell spots the party as outsiders and begins watching them over the course of a session or two. He approaches cautiously — not with information, just a question. Has the party noticed anything unusual about the bounties they've been given? He frames it as idle curiosity. He is not idle.

**Mid Act One:** If the party engages him, he shows them the list. Seven titles over the past eight months. None of them catalogued. All approved by a senior signature he can't quite verify. He doesn't know what it means — he just knows his catalogue is correct and these requests aren't.

**Act Two onward:** Pell becomes a quiet ongoing resource. He flags new irregular requests as they come in and passes them to the party. He is terrified but committed. He has decided that knowing and doing nothing makes him complicit.

> [!warning] DM Note The book titles on Pell's list should connect to Keeper operations the party has already encountered or will encounter in upcoming mysteries. Let him be a retroactive confirmation — things the party half-noticed suddenly clicking into place.

---

### Relationship to the Others

- **Thessaly Vorne** — They know each other from occasional crossover work. Thessaly has requested copies from the Binder before. Cordial, professional, nothing more.
- **Caelix Wren** — Pell attends Oghma services irregularly. He knows Caelix by face and name. They have exchanged perhaps a dozen real conversations in five years.

---

### DM Notes

> [!note] Things to Remember
> 
> - Pell is the gentlest entry point into the conspiracy. Don't make his first approach feel dangerous — it should feel like a nervous man asking a careful question.
> - His skill is noticing things in _texts_ — he will keep finding more as the campaign progresses. He's a long-term asset, not a one-scene NPC.
> - If the Keepers become aware of him, he is extremely vulnerable. He has no combat ability and no powerful allies. His safety becomes a party concern.
> - Consider having him almost mention something to Janussi early on — and then describe Janussi dismissing it in a way that felt slightly wrong. Pell didn't push it. He's been thinking about that ever since.

## Thessaly Vorne

_Female human | Early 40s | Archivist, Great Library lower stacks | Arrives: Act Two_

---

### Appearance & Personality

Thessaly is tall, angular, and perpetually slightly dusty. She wears her grey-streaked brown hair in a practical knot and favours dark robes that don't show ink stains. She has the look of someone who considers most conversations an interruption and most people a mild inconvenience — not out of cruelty, but out of genuine focus. Her work is her world.

She is not frightened in the way Pell is frightened. She is _furious_. Her catalogue is being interfered with. Her life's work is being undermined. The anger came before the fear, and it's still louder.

> [!note] At the Table Thessaly does not soften her words. She states things plainly and expects the same in return. She has little patience for vagueness or hedging. If she's brought the party information, it's because she has verified it — she will not speculate. If she doesn't know something she'll say so, sharply.

---

### Role in Candlekeep

Thessaly has spent twenty years cataloguing the oldest and most obscure sections of the Great Library — the parts most Avowed never visit, the parts that require a specific kind of patience and expertise. She knows her stacks the way a shepherd knows their flock. She notices when something is missing before she even checks the record.

Certain texts she catalogued years ago have been quietly reclassified or relocated without any official record of the change. The paperwork simply doesn't exist. Someone with very high-level access has been moving books and erasing the trail — not sloppily, but not perfectly either. Not perfectly enough for Thessaly.

She has been building a counter-record in private for four months. She hasn't told anyone because she doesn't know who has the access level to have done this — and that means she doesn't know who to trust inside the keep.

---

### What She's Noticed — Timeline

**Act Two introduction:** Thessaly approaches the party after hearing through Pell — cautiously, through an intermediary — that they are people who ask questions and can be trusted with uncomfortable answers. She is not warm about it. She presents her counter-record and lays out what she knows with the precision of someone who has rehearsed this conversation and is irritated it's necessary.

**Ongoing through Act Two:** She continues updating her counter-record and passes findings to the party. She also begins noticing a pattern in _which_ texts are being moved — they share a thematic thread she can't quite articulate yet. Something about the catacombs. Something old.

**Late Act Two / Early Act Three:** She identifies that the relocated texts all contain references — oblique, partial — to something stored beneath the keep. She doesn't know what. She brings this to the party with characteristic bluntness: _"I don't know what they're looking for. But I know where they think it is."_

> [!warning] DM Note Thessaly's counter-record is the connective tissue between Pell's irregular copy requests and the Keepers' actual goal. Her arc leads the party toward the Liber Infinitus before they know it has a name. Handle her discovery carefully — she's the bridge between Act Two's conspiracy and Act Three's revelation.

---

### Relationship to the Others

- **Pell Ashwick** — She has a professional respect for him. He's thorough. She would not have approached the party without his indirect vouching.
- **Caelix Wren** — She finds priests faintly tiresome but has nothing against Caelix specifically. They have worked in adjacent areas of the keep long enough to nod at each other in hallways.

---

### DM Notes

> [!note] Things to Remember
> 
> - Thessaly's anger is a feature, not a flaw. It makes her credible — she's not scared into action, she's offended into action.
> - She is methodical. She will not bring the party anything she hasn't verified. This makes her information more reliable than anyone else's — and slower to arrive.
> - She has no idea about the plague, Xanthoria, or Janussi's compromise. She has found the _what_ without finding the _why_. That gap is important.
> - If she ever suspects Janussi is involved she will go very quiet and very still. She has worked under him for fifteen years. That will cost her something.

## Caelix Wren

_Male half-elf | Early 50s | Priest of Oghma, Temple of Oghma | Arrives: Act Three_

---

### Appearance & Personality

Caelix is the kind of man who looks exactly like what he is — a quiet, devoted priest who has found his place and settled into it completely. Silver-templed, soft-spoken, with the unhurried manner of someone who has made peace with most things. He wears simple robes and a small bronze symbol of Oghma at his collar. His hands are steady.

He is not a dramatic person. He did not want to be involved in anything dramatic. He is here because his god is the god of knowledge, and someone is doing something to knowledge that feels, to him, like a desecration. He has run out of ways to pretend he didn't see what he saw.

> [!note] At the Table Caelix speaks slowly and chooses his words with care — not from uncertainty, but from habit. He is a man who has spent decades listening before speaking. He will not be rushed. If the party pushes him he simply waits until they stop. He is also, quietly, the most courageous of the three — he has the least investigative skill and the most personal exposure, and he came anyway.

---

### Role in Candlekeep

Caelix has served Oghma quietly for decades alongside Lorekeeper Kei Tigersteel at the Temple of Oghma. Like Kei, he has the rare privilege of entering the Great Library unaccompanied — a trust built over thirty years of faithful service.

He uses this access primarily to pray at certain alcoves deep in the Inner Ward, away from the bustle of scholars and scribes. It is a practice he has kept for twenty years. He knows those alcoves the way he knows his own hands.

Lately the alcoves show signs of use by someone who wasn't there to pray. Disturbed dust where no one should have walked. Faint footprints leading toward the wall rather than away from it. Once — three weeks ago — a scrap of parchment bearing a symbol he had never seen before, tucked beneath the base of a shelf. He took it. He has been carrying it ever since.

He does not know what the symbol means. He knows it does not belong there. And he knows — with the certainty of a man who has spent his life in a place of knowledge — that someone has found a way to move through the Inner Ward without being seen.

---

### What He's Noticed — Timeline

**Act Three introduction:** By the time Caelix approaches the party, things have escalated enough that he cannot justify staying silent. He has heard rumours — through the quiet channels of temple life — that something is badly wrong. He comes to the party because he knows who they are by now, because Pell told him they could be trusted, and because he has carried that scrap of parchment for weeks and needs someone to take it from him.

**The Symbol:** The symbol on the scrap of parchment is the Keepers of Forbidden Lore seal — the same one the party found in the safe house during Act Two. This is the confirmation that the Keepers have penetrated all the way to the Inner Ward itself.

**His access:** Caelix's unaccompanied library access becomes critically useful in Act Three. He can move through areas the party cannot reach without escort, and he is willing to use that access carefully on their behalf.

> [!warning] DM Note Caelix's arrival signals the conspiracy is no longer operating on the edges of Candlekeep — it's inside the heart of it. His scrap of parchment connecting to the safe house symbol should land as a genuine shock. The Keepers have been in the Inner Ward. How long? What have they seen? What have they taken?

---

### Relationship to the Others

- **Pell Ashwick** — Caelix is fond of Pell in a gentle, avuncular way. He has been the one who quietly told Pell the party could be trusted, which is why Pell approached them in the first place. He has been watching from a distance longer than anyone realised.
- **Thessaly Vorne** — He respects her competence and finds her abrasiveness almost restful — she never says anything she doesn't mean. They understand each other without being close.

---

### DM Notes

> [!note] Things to Remember
> 
> - Caelix is the emotional anchor of the three. Pell is nervous, Thessaly is furious — Caelix is _sad_. He loves Candlekeep. The idea that something has been corrupting it quietly for years grieves him.
> - His unaccompanied Inner Ward access is a significant practical asset in Act Three. Don't waste it.
> - He carries the scrap of parchment with the Keepers' symbol. When he hands it to the party, let that beat land. It connects everything.
> - If Janussi's compromise is revealed while Caelix is present, watch how you play his reaction. He and Janussi have served Candlekeep at the same time for thirty years. They are not close friends — but they are not strangers either.
> - Caelix will want to be present for whatever ending comes. He will want to see Candlekeep restored. Let him.

---

## When They Come Together

The three informants do not coordinate until the party connects them. That moment — when Pell, Thessaly, and Caelix are in the same room comparing notes for the first time — is a scene worth staging deliberately.

> [!note] The Convergence Scene Consider engineering a moment in Act Three where the party brings all three together. They have been working in parallel, seeing the same conspiracy from different angles, and none of them have spoken to the others about it. Put them in a room — one of the silence-spelled chambers in the Pillars of Pedagogy would be appropriate — and let them compare notes. The cumulative picture should be significantly more complete than any one of them realised. This scene is also where the party sees, for the first time, how frightened these three ordinary people actually are. Use it.
