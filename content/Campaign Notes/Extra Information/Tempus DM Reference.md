## God Overview

| | |
|---|---|
| **Alignment** | Chaotic Neutral |
| **Symbol** | Red shield with a yellow flaming sword |
| **Holy Day** | Feast of the Moon — honouring the fallen |
| **Allies** | Red Knight (exarch/"daughter"), Valkur |
| **Enemies** | Garagos (rival war god), Eldath (philosophical opposition) |

Tempus is the greater god of war — one of the mightiest in the Realms by sheer number of worshippers. He answers to no one and holds no fixed allegiances. Temples resemble military fortresses: barracks, mess halls, armories, training grounds. The church has no central authority — impossible given they field priests on both sides of conflicts — but individual temples maintain strict military hierarchy.

> [!note] Tempus & Eldath
> Despite their philosophical opposition, Tempus commands his followers not to harm Eldath's faithful. His reasoning: war has no meaning if peace does not exist to define it.

## Rituals & Observances

### Daily
- **Highsun prayer** — clerics prepare spells; Feast of Heroes held at midday
- **Sunset** — Song of the Fallen, honouring the dead
- **After dark** — Song of the Sword ceremony, open to lay worshippers

### Weekly
- **Blood tithe** — once per tenday, the faithful must spill blood (their own or a worthy enemy's) and sing the Song of the Sword. This is literal, not metaphorical.

### Annual
- **March of the Dead** — priests walk through town calling citizens to the Feast of the Moon. Names of the battle-fallen are chanted. War priests are charged with preserving these names on gravestones, prayer scrolls, and in temple records.

### Pre-Battle
A prayer spoken over the warrior's primary weapon, requesting valour and survival. If a new weapon was taken as battle-loot before the fight, it replaces the regular weapon for this rite — considered a sign of Tempus's direct favour.

> [!note] Passphrase
> "Tempus thanks you" / "And I thank Tempus" — spoken between faithful to acknowledge a worthy deed. Useful as an in-world recognition signal between Tempurans who don't know each other.

## What the Church Does (and Doesn't Do)

- Serves as wartime chaplains for any army — they do not take sides, only uphold honour
- Will **not** act as generals or commanders-for-hire — they advise on honour, not strategy
- Maintains forges, training grounds, and armories — often the best smiths in a region
- Arms those with genuine need for battle, including enemies facing a greater threat
- Preserves the names of the fallen — every temple is also a war memorial
- Does **not** tolerate cowardice, dishonourable kills, attacks on non-combatants, or feuds beyond a settled dispute

## The Order of the Steel Fang

### Formation & Original Purpose
Founded formally in 1352 DR, growing out of an informal strike force called the Steel Fang of the God. Originally served as elite protection — bodyguards for royal heirs and escorts for sacred relics in transit between temples.

### The Corruption
Over the following century, wealthy nobles began hiring the Steel Fang in increasingly aggressive roles. What started as protection crept into offense. The church's coffers filled; senior clergy grew uneasy. Tempus himself was displeased.

The order became embedded in the petty wars of Chessenta and the Vilhon Reach — noble heirs feuding over succession, barons annexing neighbours. The Steel Fang was on every side, collecting fees and losing its identity as a holy order.

### The Tempuswrath (1466 DR)
The breaking point: two groups of Steel Fangs, hired by opposing patrons, met on the same battlefield and began killing each other. Fang against Fang — for coin, for lords who had no concept of what they had purchased.

> [!warning] Key Event — The Tempuswrath
> Tempus manifested physically on the field. He ignored all weapons and spells directed at him. He personally slew the patrons who had hired the Fang members, then addressed his warriors directly: *"Go home to your altars, and leave these leaderless folk to fight their own battles."* He vanished in a roar of thunder. Every combatant stopped. The conflict ended.
>
> The Tempuswrath is now one of the most significant moments in recent Tempuran history. It is spoken of at every Steel Fang induction. It is the reason the order exists as it does today.

### The Reformation (1467 DR — Present)
The order was restructured entirely and no longer accepts outside contracts. It now operates exclusively as:

- An internal disciplinary and enforcement force for the Church of Tempus
- A strike force for missions the church deems too dangerous for standard priests
- A protective escort for sacred objects, relics, and persons of critical importance to the faith
- Hunters of schisms, heresies, and those who dishonour Tempus's name within the church

As of 1492 DR (campaign timeline), the Steel Fang is small, elite, and trusted. Members answer directly to senior church leadership and operate with significant autonomy. They do not travel in armies — they travel in small, precise units, or alone.

## DM Hooks

### For Toothsome
- [ ] Is he on an active Steel Fang mission, or has he been cut loose — and why?
- [ ] How does he personally carry the shame of the Tempuswrath? Quiet resolve? Rigid honour? Overcompensation?
- [ ] Does he have a handler within the church? Could that handler become a pressure point?
- [ ] Has he gone somewhat rogue — still faithful, but no longer under direct orders? What caused the split?

### For the Campaign
- [ ] The Steel Fang's enforcement role means the church could send other Fangs after Toothsome if he acts against church interests
- [ ] Tempuran temples near the Sword Coast: the House of Heroes (Waterdeep) and a temple in Luskan — potential contact points or sources of orders
- [ ] A mission to recover a stolen relic, hunt a heretic Tempuran, or protect a church-designated VIP could all tie naturally into his backstory
- [ ] If Steel Fangs ever end up on opposing sides of a conflict within the campaign, the Tempuswrath looms — deeply uncomfortable, with serious divine stakes

> [!note] Tone Reminder
> Tempus priests are not paladins. They don't preach. They don't recruit. They fight, remember the dead, and uphold the rules of war. A Steel Fang member should feel like a special operator — quiet, precise, deeply experienced, carrying a complex history. Not a lawful good crusader. Something more morally textured.
