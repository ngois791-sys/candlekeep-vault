# Orris Vane
_Keeper Agent  —  Vault Access | Great Library, Candlekeep_

> [!important] DM Eyes Only Orris Vane is a willing Keeper agent and the only one of the four who cannot be redeemed. He is not confused, blackmailed, or deceived. He knows exactly what he serves and has constructed an airtight ideological framework that accommodates any evidence the party can present. Even confronted with Xanthoria's true nature, he will find a way to justify it. He is the wall.

## Appearance

Thin to the point of angularity, with long fingers perpetually ink-stained at the tips and a posture developed from thirty years of leaning over manuscripts. His hair is a close-cropped steel gray, neat to the point of severity. His eyes are pale hazel and very sharp — the eyes of a man who reads everything twice and believes almost nothing on first pass.

He dresses in Avowed robes that are immaculate but old — he has worn the same style for decades and sees no reason to change. He carries a leather folio everywhere, filled with classification notes and reclassification orders in his precise, tiny handwriting.

**Voice:** Dry, deliberate, and unhurried. He speaks in complete sentences, never trails off, and pauses before answering questions as though running them through a mental checklist. He does not raise his voice. Ever.

---

## Who He Is — The Truth

Orris Vane has worked in Candlekeep's restricted vaults for thirty-two years. He came here as a young scholar who genuinely believed in the library's mission. He still does — he simply believes Candlekeep has failed to live up to it.

His position is this: Candlekeep preserves knowledge. It does not _protect_ the world from knowledge. Any moderately resourced scholar can gain access to texts describing plague rituals, void entities, catacomb systems of ancient keeps, and the locations of artifacts that could end cities — provided they bring a rare enough book to the gate. The Avowed call this open access a virtue. Orris calls it negligence dressed up as principle.

The Keepers found him eighteen years ago through a paper he published — a dry, technical argument that Candlekeep's reclassification protocols were insufficient to prevent dangerous knowledge from reaching unsuitable hands. They agreed with him. They offered him the opportunity to do something about it.

He has been doing something about it ever since.

**What he has done:**

- Systematically reclassified texts relating to Candlekeep's founding, the catacombs, Miirym, and the Chamber of Lost Lore — moving them deeper into restricted access, requiring higher clearance, making them progressively harder to find for casual researchers
- Approved copy requests from Keeper agents while losing the paperwork trail
- Flagged specific scholars to the Keepers when their research was getting too close to sensitive subjects
- Facilitated the disappearance of at least three texts that were reclassified so deeply they effectively ceased to exist in the public catalogue

**What he doesn't know:** He knows the Keepers have interests beneath Candlekeep. He believes their goal is to properly custodise what lies there — to put the right people in charge of the most dangerous knowledge in the world. He does not know about the Saprophytic Plague. He does not know Xanthoria is a lich. But here is what makes Orris different from the others: if he finds out, he will not be horrified. He will recalculate. He will decide whether a lich with the right philosophy is preferable to a world of scholars with no philosophy at all. There is a meaningful chance he decides it is.

## What He Does for the Keepers

- **Reclassification** — his primary tool. A text moved from the open stacks to restricted access to deep vault to "missing" is a text that no longer exists for most of the world. He has been doing this for eighteen years, methodically.
- **Copy request approval** — he controls which copy requests from the House of the Binder get processed and which get quietly shelved. Several texts the Keepers wanted duplicates of have been copied and the records destroyed.
- **Flagging researchers** — when a scholar's requests form a pattern pointing toward the catacombs, Miirym, or pre-Candlekeep history, Orris notes it and passes the name upward.
- **The Janussi signatures** — he was not involved in forging Janussi's signature (that was Ramilir). But he approved the copy requests after the fact, retroactively legitimising the paperwork.

---

## What He Knows

- More than any other agent inside Candlekeep. He has been active for eighteen years, has a high security clearance within the Keeper network, and has asked uncomfortable questions that his handlers have answered honestly because he's too valuable to lose.
- He knows the Keepers want access to the catacombs and specifically to the Chamber of Lost Lore.
- He knows Miirym is the primary obstacle.
- He knows Janussi has been "influenced" — he believes this is ideological persuasion. He has not asked further.
- He knows several agent identities, including Serelyth and Kei Tigersteel.
- He knows the Keeper seal and the full recognition protocol.
- He does not know Xanthoria's name, nature, or existence.

## How He's Discovered

**Thessaly Vorne** (Act Two informant, lower stacks archivist) notices that reclassification orders occasionally bypass the standard two-signature approval chain. One archivist's name appears on nearly all of them: Orris Vane. She doesn't know what it means — she just knows the paperwork is wrong. She brings it to the party.

From there, the party has to build a case. Orris is careful and has been doing this for eighteen years. The trail exists but it's deliberately obscured.

**Investigation options:**

- Cross-referencing reclassification logs against Keeper fingerprint moments (e.g., the texts Matreous was studying before his death have since been reclassified — by Orris)
- Catching a copy request that Orris approved that has no corresponding record in the Binder's logs
- Thessaly can identify three specific reclassification orders she flagged internally that were overridden — all by Orris, all within a week of a Keeper-connected event

---

## The Confrontation

Orris Vane does not panic, does not bluster, and does not beg. When cornered with sufficient evidence, he acknowledges it. Calmly. He then explains his position with the same dry precision he applies to everything.

He believes what he did was right. He will argue it clearly and without apology. He will point out that the party has benefitted from Candlekeep's "open access" while pursuing a conspiracy that has resulted in murders, plagues, and the corruption of the Keeper of Tomes — and that none of that would have happened if dangerous knowledge had been properly controlled from the beginning.

He is not entirely wrong. That's what makes him the hardest conversation in the campaign.

**If shown evidence of Xanthoria:** He goes quiet. He thinks. He says: _"A lich is a problem of implementation, not of principle."_ He genuinely believes this. He will not help the party. He will not actively hinder them either — he is not a fighter and he knows it. But he will not turn.

**What the party can do with him:**

- Turn him over to the Avowed (he will be tried and imprisoned; he accepts this without drama)
- Leave him in place as an unwilling, watched source of information (risky — he may alert the Keepers)
- He cannot be flipped. Do not offer him the option. He'll refuse and it will tell the Keepers the party is onto them.

## Roleplaying Notes

- He is the most intellectually honest villain in the campaign. He doesn't lie about what he's done or why. This makes him genuinely uncomfortable to talk to.
- He has no personal cruelty. He doesn't enjoy what he does — he considers it administrative necessity.
- He has a habit of correcting minor factual errors in other people's statements, even mid-interrogation. It's not smugness. It's reflex.
- He keeps a small personal library in his office — texts he considers too dangerous for any catalogue, which he has simply taken. He has read all of them.
- If the party tries to appeal to his better nature, he will listen politely and then explain why their framing is emotionally rather than logically driven. He's not being cruel. He's being Orris.
